## Strongest Performance vs. Benchmark

1. **Treatment Retention (overall: 70.1% vs. 55% national benchmark, +15.1 pp).** Headway's retention rate across all diagnoses and payers is 15 percentage points above the SAMHSA national average for 90-day outpatient mental health retention. This is a standout metric and a powerful proof point for payer conversations.

2. **Sessions to Clinically Significant Change in MDD (7.5 vs. 8.2 sessions, 0.7 sessions faster).** Headway patients with Major Depressive Disorder reach clinically significant improvement nearly a full session sooner than the APA practice-network benchmark. This translates to faster time-to-value and lower cost per episode for payers.

3. **Median GAD-7 Improvement Exceeds Clinically Meaningful Threshold.** Headway's GAD patients show a median 5.5-point GAD-7 reduction, well above the 4-point threshold that defines clinically meaningful change in the literature. While the benchmark is rate-based (48% achieving the threshold), the aggregate median exceeding 4 points is a strong signal.

---

## Greatest Room for Improvement

1. **PHQ-9 Reduction in MDD (-5.8 vs. -6.4 points, 0.6 points below benchmark).** Headway's weighted average median PHQ-9 improvement for MDD patients falls 0.6 points short of the CBT meta-analytic benchmark. This gap is modest but clinically notable, especially since the benchmark reflects structured 12-16 session CBT protocols while Headway patients improve in 7.5 sessions on average. Still, closing this gap would strengthen the clinical narrative.

2. **GAD Retention vs. Structured CBT Protocols (70.6% vs. 83%, -12.4 pp).** When compared specifically to structured CBT protocol retention benchmarks for anxiety, Headway's GAD retention trails by over 12 percentage points. The benchmark population is enrolled in formal research-protocol CBT, which may have higher inherent engagement, but the gap warrants attention.

3. **Medicaid Retention (57.3% vs. 55% national average, only +2.3 pp).** While Headway's Medicaid retention still exceeds the national benchmark, the margin is slim compared to the 15+ point advantage seen in commercial populations. Medicaid patients face distinct access barriers, and this population (n=2,209) represents an area where targeted intervention could improve outcomes.

## Executive Narrative

**Headline: Headway's retention rates substantially outperform national benchmarks, and patients reach meaningful improvement faster than published norms -- but symptom reduction depth and Medicaid equity deserve focused investment.**

Across 101,814 patients in Q4 2025 spanning six major payers and eight states, Headway demonstrates strong clinical performance on two dimensions that matter most to payer partners and patients: keeping people in care and getting them better quickly. Our 70.1% treatment retention rate is 15 percentage points above the SAMHSA national average of 55%, a gap that holds across Major Depressive Disorder, Generalized Anxiety Disorder, and all commercial populations. Patients with MDD reach clinically significant change in 7.5 sessions on average, nearly a full session faster than the APA practice-network benchmark of 8.2 sessions. These results reinforce Headway's value proposition: patients stay engaged and improve efficiently.

The data also reveals areas for focused improvement. PHQ-9 reduction in MDD (-5.8 points) falls 0.6 points below the meta-analytic benchmark for structured CBT (-6.4 points). This gap is partially explained by protocol differences -- the benchmark reflects intensive 12-16 session CBT in controlled settings, while Headway's network operates in real-world conditions with shorter average episode lengths. Nonetheless, this is a metric worth closing, and potential levers include broader adoption of measurement-based care, structured treatment protocols, and therapist coaching on evidence-based techniques.

Medicaid patients represent a clear equity concern. While their 57.3% retention rate still exceeds the national average, it trails Headway's commercial retention by 13 percentage points. With only 2,209 Medicaid patients in the dataset, the sample is also relatively small. Expanding Medicaid access while improving engagement -- through proactive outreach, reduced scheduling friction, and addressing social determinants of care continuity -- should be a strategic priority.

**Data caveats:** These results rely on aggregate medians and weighted averages across heterogeneous populations; patient-level distributions are not visible in this dataset. Several benchmarks are rate-based (e.g., percentage achieving a threshold) while Headway data reports median point changes, limiting exact apples-to-apples comparison. Benchmarks span different years (2001-2024) and populations (clinical trials, national surveys, practice networks), and may not perfectly reflect Headway's patient mix. PTSD outcomes (n=5,611) currently lack external benchmarks entirely.

**Recommended Focus Areas for Next Quarter:**

1. **Close the PHQ-9 depth gap.** Investigate whether measurement-based care adoption and structured session planning can move MDD symptom reduction closer to the -6.4 benchmark. Consider a pilot with a cohort of high-volume therapists.
2. **Improve Medicaid retention.** Design targeted engagement interventions for Medicaid populations, including appointment reminders, no-show follow-up, and care navigator support. Set a Q1 target of 62% retention for this segment.
3. **Establish PTSD benchmarks.** Source validated external benchmarks for PTSD treatment outcomes (e.g., PCL-5 response rates, VA/DoD clinical practice guideline targets) and integrate them into the next quarterly review to ensure this growing population receives appropriate performance scrutiny.
