### Thought Leadership Draft

**Word count target: 600–800 words**

---

We have spent a decade building a mental health system that counts sessions delivered and calls it progress. It is not progress. A system that cannot tell you whether the person on the other side of the therapeutic relationship is actually getting better is not a care system — it is a billing system with good intentions.

That era is ending, and the evidence is arriving from multiple directions at once. A landmark randomized controlled trial published in *JAMA Psychiatry* this month showed that measurement-based care — the simple, systematic practice of tracking patient symptom scores and using them to guide clinical decisions — improves depression outcomes by 30 percent compared to treatment as usual. CMS is now proposing to require behavioral health outcome reporting, including PHQ-9 response rates, in Medicare Advantage value-based contracts. The signal is unmistakable: the industry must move from volume to value, and it must do so with data.

At Headway, we have been building toward this moment for years. Our measurement-based care program recently passed one million validated PHQ-9 and GAD-7 assessments — making us one of the largest sources of real-world mental health outcome data in the country. That is not a vanity metric. It is the foundation for understanding what actually works: which treatment approaches produce durable improvement, which patient-provider matches lead to better outcomes, and where the system is failing the people it is meant to serve. Our most recent internal outcome analysis shows that 62 percent of Headway patients with depression achieve clinically meaningful improvement within 12 sessions, a figure that exceeds published benchmarks. We share these numbers not to boast, but because transparency about outcomes is exactly what this industry has been missing.

But outcomes data without clinical integrity is meaningless. The Cerebral settlement this month — $15 million to resolve allegations of improper prescribing and inadequate clinical oversight — is a cautionary tale for every digital health company operating at the intersection of speed and scale. Growth is not a strategy if it outpaces your capacity for clinical governance. At Headway, we recently implemented an updated prescribing oversight protocol that requires chart review of five percent of prescribing encounters every month and mandatory case consultation for controlled substance prescriptions. We have also invested in an evidence-based treatment matching algorithm that achieves 84 percent concordance with expert panel recommendations. These are not headline-grabbing features. They are the structural commitments that make everything else trustworthy.

The third challenge is the hardest and the most urgent: access. A recent KFF investigation found that Medicaid patients in rural areas wait an average of 47 days for an initial mental health appointment. Bureau of Labor Statistics data shows therapist demand outpacing supply by three to one in most states. These are not statistics; they are people in crisis being told to wait. Headway now has over 40,000 active providers across all 50 states, and we are not stopping at commercially insured populations. We recently launched our first Medicaid managed care pilot with Centene in Texas and Florida, because closing the access gap means going where the need is greatest, not where the margins are most comfortable. Paired with our enrollment in multi-state telehealth licensure compacts, we are working to ensure that geography and insurance type stop being determinants of whether someone can see a therapist.

There is a version of the future where mental health care remains fragmented — where outcomes are opaque, clinical governance is an afterthought, and access is rationed by zip code and payer. There is another version where we use the infrastructure, data, and clinical discipline we are building now to create something genuinely different: a system that knows whether it is working, holds itself accountable when it is not, and reaches every person who needs it. We are building the second version. The question for the rest of the industry is whether it is willing to do the same.
